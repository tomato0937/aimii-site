# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

EduBaania serves parents of upper-elementary to junior-high students who are worried that their child is working hard but not improving in a visible, reliable way. These parents want less guessing, less daily conflict, and a clearer way to understand where their child is stuck.

Secondary users are the students themselves. They should experience the service as supportive and directional: they know what to do next, they can see small wins, and they are not shamed for not understanding something yet.

## Product Purpose

EduBaania is a K-12 self-learning outcome operating system. It helps families move from vague anxiety to a clear learning loop: diagnose the real learning gaps, prescribe the next route, execute daily tasks, track progress, and report what changed.

The primary public-site conversion is the 15-minute AI learning checkup. The checkup should feel low-pressure, useful even before purchase, and valuable because it shows the child's knowledge gaps, error patterns, learning behavior, and recommended next steps.

## Brand Personality

Credible, warm, and clear.

The brand should feel like a calm learning partner for families: professional enough for parents to trust, human enough for children to feel safe, and clear enough that the next step never feels abstract.

## Anti-references

- Not an AI tutor landing page that only sells smart technology.
- Not a cram-school advertisement built around pressure, guarantees, or fear.
- Not a generic online app, worksheet bank, or dashboard demo.
- Not a cheap pile of rounded cards, oversized icons, and repeated template sections.
- Not investor language, market sizing, technical boasting, or internal business-model copy.
- Do not publicly lead with offline space, OMO, flagship-store language, pricing bands, or operational infrastructure.

## Design Principles

- Every major page section should answer: what is the parent worried about, how does EduBaania handle it, and what evidence can the parent see?
- Lead with outcomes parents can feel: fewer blind spots, less parent-child conflict, clearer next steps, and visible weekly progress.
- Use imagery and system mocks to make the invisible learning loop concrete. Dashboards, reports, task flows, and family learning scenes should support the message rather than decorate it.
- Keep the voice concise and parent-facing. Avoid abstract slogans when a concrete learning situation is clearer.
- Preserve a premium, international EdTech feel: bright white space, teal/coral/sky accents, refined iconography, careful alignment, and generous but purposeful rhythm.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA for contrast, focus states, and keyboard access. Support reduced motion. Maintain readable typography, touch targets of at least 44px, and no horizontal overflow on mobile.

Avoid language that blames children or parents. Progress should be framed as understandable, measurable, and possible.
